Research

As a National Institutes of Health (NIH) Ruth L. Kirschstein T32 Fellow in Trauma and Sepsis, my work is focused on understanding the biological mechanisms underpinning poor prognoses for trauma patients. In other words, who gets sickest the fastest following traumatic injury, and why? Moreover, how can we quickly identify these patients and adapt their care in order to improve outcomes?

I am particularly interested in leveraging the high-quality prehospital data obtained by ground and helicopter Pittsburgh emergency medical services (EMS) agencies, clinical data collected at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center (UPMC), and biological time series measurements (e.g. key inflammatory mediators) to identify predictors of outcomes for trauma and sepsis patients. This involves secondary analyses and the application of machine learning techniques to data from studies such as PAMPer (Sperry et al., 2018, NEJM). The goal is to better understand how prehospital interventions affect the biology of the severely-injured polytrauma patient. My work is based out of the Billiar Lab and mentored by investigators from the Department of Surgery and the Department of Emergency Medicine.

Pittsburgh integrates a number of outstanding EMS agencies, experts in pre- and in-hospital trauma and shock, and computational power at both the University of Pittsburgh and Carnegie Mellon University. I am fortunate to collaborate with colleagues across these organizations.

Training

I received a PhD in Biogeochemistry from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution (WHOI). Using experimental and computational approaches, I investigated novel stable isotope and phylogenetic signals diagnostic of modern and ancient microbial metabolisms (read more). This work resulted in a number of presentations and publications (Google Scholar). Some related code and data is on GitHub. My work was generously supported by an NSF GRFP fellowship and a Shell - MIT Energy Initiative fellowship.

I also completed the Harvard-MIT Health Sciences & Technology Graduate Education in Medical Sciences Training Program.  I led a project between Harvard Brigham and Women's Hospital, MIT, the National Collegiate Emergency Medical Services Foundation, and the Massachusetts Department of Public Health that aimed to assess the mental health of college students and collegiate EMS providers in order to reduce barriers to care.
